About
All children are natural philosophers. Born into a world of unknown, they have a ceaseless desire to understand. I sometimes question what happens to that curiosity. Do we just grow out of it? Or do we reach a point of contentment with what we know? I still feel that the same wonderment we have as kids is still there, somewhere. And if so, this site is dedicated to that part.

Before I go any further, I want to provide a brief introduction and sort of an explanation for what this is. Hello, my name is Callum. Like many others, when I was young, I was possessed with a natural inquisitiveness for things. At age 8 and upon my request, my mum bought me a telescope. As she warily scrubbed the dishes at night, I would sit outside on the deck with laminated print-outs and the hope of spotting something recognisable beyond the clouds. And, as I helped my mum fold the laundry, I would taper off into facts about black holes, how sound travels through space, and gravity keeps our moon in orbit. This continued up to the age of 12 when I started taking science classes. I thought I would have a bit of an edge but the truth was that I really struggled. We started of with biology and my teacher would list of the mechanics of life as if he was reading a shopping list. We'd dive into something really specific for a 12 year old, something like cell production and as he continued to blister through the topics, I was still trying to grasp how scientist even found these tiny things. It felt to me as if we were trying to read a book by starting at page 70 and then leaping all over the place. A few years later, I was tackling organic compounds. The teacher would continously break away from the white board and tell us to "write the following". He would then wander around the class composing his sentences with a conceited smugness as we furiously scribbled down as much as we could with little regard for comprehension. Now, my teacher's did understand their stuff, but it was like they couldn't remember what it was like in a world before they had acquired their understanding. My education therefore seemed to be sculpted almost only by a single tool: forceful repetition; and the goal of achieving some level of imitation from it. It was like assembling a car engine with just a hammer and a few pictures of the finished product. But I didn't want to learn how to use just a hammer, I wanted to explore other tools for learning and for understanding. And I didn't want to learn how to just make cars, I wanted to make rockets.

It was at university, when I was faced once again with a similar situation, that my friend and I first coined the notion of learning through "first principles". It was taken from an expression used in calculus. At the time I was learning about PID controllers and signal processing by professors who were perhaps more invested and better suited to research. So, as we shuffled through slides we tried looking for a different angle that could shine a better light on the topic. Simply put, the idea of "first principles" was less about focusing on the content and more about getting a better understanding of the people. What were they trying to do at the time? What did they already know? By establishing a basline of understanding that doesn't make any radical assumptions, we then tried to learn something by simply rediscovering it. And so, passionately curious is about taking some of the fascinating things that permeate our world and explaining them in the fashion of a simple story, one through the eyes of the discoverers.

Contact
In German, the verb for teaching and the verb for learning are the same word. Althought this was most likely a societal development, if it were the decision of a single person, I would like to believe that that person realised that teaching and learning are inseparable friends. You see, as we learn things, we develop a desire to pass them on to others. Equally so, as we try teach we develop a much more comprehensive understanding of that subject. Therefore, as an advocate of this two-way relationship, I believe it is important that you have the opportunity to communicate your ideas with me. So below I have placed a contact form for you to feel free to express your criticism, appreciation or confusion.
